The brief

An unfinished structure, held up by temporary props

The staircase at the heart of this residence was built and installed before CAworks was involved. When our engineer first walked the site, the helix stood only because scaffolding and auxiliary props were carrying its load and restraining it from twisting.

Under controlled walking tests the structure showed global vibration, perceptible torsional flex and audible joint movement. There was no central column and no clear, continuous load path; the segmented stringers had been welded together on site with no weld procedures, no inspection records and no verification of the anchors fixing it to the slabs.

Our remit was unambiguous: assess it honestly, then engineer, document and rebuild it into a permanent staircase that is structurally sound, fully compliant with the Dubai Building Code, and worthy of the architecture around it.

Step 01 — Site inspection

Seeing the problem before touching it

Every rescue starts with a non-intrusive inspection. We record the as-found geometry and behaviour, walk the structure under load, and listen — joint noise and movement tell you as much as deflection does.

Critically, we do not remove the temporary supports. At this stage the props are the only thing preventing torsional rotation and overload of the anchors, so they stay in place until an engineered solution is designed and built.

Step 02 — Professional opinion

A written, signed structural opinion

Before proposing any fix, CAworks issued a formal Professional Opinion — Condition Assessment & Structural Adequacy Review, prepared by a chartered engineer. It documents what was observed, classifies the risk, and sets out conceptual reinforcement strategies — without yet committing to a design.

The conclusion was direct: in its as-found configuration the staircase was structurally unstable without its temporary supports, with a real risk of progressive connection failure if they were removed. The full document is reproduced below with client details removed.

Step 03 — Concept options

Five ways to make it stand on its own

Rather than jump to one answer, we set out the realistic reinforcement strategies, each with its structural logic and its impact on the architecture — so the design conversation could be had on engineering terms.

Option A — Lateral fixing to structural walls

Rigid connections tie the stringers back to the reinforced-concrete walls, cutting the cantilever span and restraining torsion. Concealed behind cladding to suit the architecture.

Option B — Central / vertical support column

One or more steel columns carry vertical load directly to the slab, all but eliminating cantilever action and global deflection. Clad or decorated to match the design intent.

Option C — Suspended rods from above

Steel tension rods hung from a new structural beam transfer vertical and torsional load upward. Effective, but dependent on new primary steel overhead.

Option D — Reposition to engage a wall

Shifting the helix to bear against a structural wall gives a direct, redundant load path without visible lateral ties — at the cost of moving the architectural set-out.

Option E — Tread stiffening + added stringer

Welded triangular stiffeners under each tread and a second outer stringer raise local rigidity. A complementary measure, recommended alongside one of the primary schemes.

The direction taken. The remediation combined a central structural mast (Ø610 × 14.3 mm CHS) with radial support arms and welded brackets — an Option B-style spine that gives the helix a clear vertical load path — reinforced by tread-level stiffening and an outer stringer anchored back to the wall. It removes the torsional instability at its root while staying invisible behind the finished treads and glass.

Step 05 — 3D laser survey

Measuring reality, not the drawing

A hand-built, segmented, site-welded staircase never matches its original drawings. Segments drift during erection, welds pull the geometry, and the surrounding slabs and walls are rarely exactly where the architect's model says. To engineer a fix that actually fits, we first capture the structure as it truly is.

A 3D laser scanner (LiDAR) sweeps the space, firing hundreds of thousands of pulses per second and timing each reflection to fix a point in space. The result is a dense point cloud — millions of measured points — that we register into a single survey-accurate model. Photogrammetry adds true-colour texture where it helps.

That captured geometry becomes the master reference for the reinforcement design, the FEA model, and the millimetre-accurate detailing of the oak treads and curved glass — eliminating guesswork and avoiding expensive re-work on site.

Step 08 — Code compliance

Reading the code the way a reviewer will

A staircase passes authority review only if it is assessed clause-by-clause against the right code. Below is the actual reasoning CAworks set out against the Dubai Building Code 2021, Part K (Villas) — shown next to the real regulation pages it relies on.

The train of thought

1 · Classify the stair correctly. This is a helical / curved stair, assessed under clause K.5.5.2.8 (spiral & curved stairs and winders). Because its tread band sits well away from the centre, we tested it against both the spiral and the curved thresholds — and it satisfies both, so the classification debate never changes the outcome.

2 · Confirm it is even permitted. K.5.5.2.1 expressly allows spiral, winder and open stairs in villas for everything except basement egress. This stair serves no basement, so no fire-rated enclosure is imposed by Part K.

3 · Separate requirements from recommendations. Intermediate landings (K.5.5.2.4) are a recommendation for villa flights over 4 m — not a requirement; the 3,660 mm between-landings limit belongs to Part B / the Fire & Life Safety Code and does not apply to villa stairs; the "spiral not recommended as a main stair" line is advisory, not a prohibition. Knowing which is which is what keeps a submission moving.

4 · Report the one genuine conflict honestly. Clause h) wants the outer handrail on the descending user's right; this helix turns the other way, placing the outer edge on the left. Handing is fixed by the direction of the spiral — it can only be "corrected" by mirroring the whole stair, which the architecture won't allow. So we record it as non-compliant as designed and propose a documented mitigation rather than hiding it.

Step 09 — Authority submission (NOC)

Why the approval has to be right — and on time

In Dubai, structural and life-safety documentation for a villa staircase is submitted through the project's Consultant of Record for authority review and a design No-Objection Certificate (NOC) before fabrication is released — and a completion certificate is needed at the end. Getting it wrong, or letting it slip, has real consequences.

01 Fabrication can't be released

If the design is rejected or queried, fabrication is held. A structure already on site stays propped, unusable and exposed — exactly the position this project started from.

02 Completion certificate withheld

Without compliant, signed-off staircase documentation, the villa's completion and handover can be blocked — delaying occupancy and final payments across the whole project.

03 Penalties & liability

Unpermitted or non-compliant structural work exposes owner and contractors to municipal penalties, invalidated insurance, and direct liability should the stair ever fail in use.

04 Rework or removal

A stair built ahead of approval may have to be modified — or removed — to satisfy the code: far costlier than engineering and evidencing it correctly the first time.

05 Programme cost

Every week an NOC is delayed pushes the entire fit-out programme, with knock-on standby and re-mobilisation costs across every trade on site.

06 How CAworks de-risks it

We issue the compliance assessment, calculations, GA drawings and glass specification as one coordinated pack — pre-empting the reviewer's questions on landings, classification and handing so the NOC is secured first time.

Step 11 — Steel fabrication

Rebuilding the steel, properly this time

The remediation steel — the central Ø610 mast, its radial arms and the load-transfer brackets — is fabricated and welded under controlled workshop conditions, a world away from the unverified site welds of the original. Heavy plate is rolled and cut to the helix, segments are trial-assembled, and every bracket is made to carry its tread's load cleanly into the central spine.

This is where decades of curved-staircase fabrication experience matters: getting a multi-segment helix to come together true, with welds that will pass inspection and perform under torsion and fatigue for the life of the building.

Step 12 — Steel installation

Erected on a clear load path

The fabricated assembly is installed against its new central mast and the upper and lower slab landings, with the outer stringer anchored back to the structural wall. Only once the engineered structure is carrying load can the original temporary props finally come out — this time with a verified, continuous load path beneath every step.

Step 13 — Tread production

Solid oak, shaped to the helix

Each step is clad in solid French oak, top- and bottom-shaped to follow the curve of the helix, finished at ~30 mm and stained or varnished to the client's selection. Treads are machined to the surveyed geometry so they seat precisely on the steel below, with consistent nosings around the full sweep of the stair.

Step 14 — Glass fabrication

How the curved balustrade is made

The balustrade is 17.5 mm curved laminated toughened glass — among the most demanding products in architectural glass. It is made in four stages:

  1. Cut & shape. Flat float glass is cut to size and the edges are ground and polished — done before any heat treatment, because toughened glass can never be re-cut.
  2. Curve. Each sheet is heated in a bending furnace until it softens and slumps onto a curved mould machined to the exact radius — here close to R980 — so the curvature is built into the glass itself.
  3. Toughen. The curved pane is rapidly air-quenched (tempered), locking the surface into compression. It becomes several times stronger and, if ever broken, crumbles into small blunt fragments instead of shards.
  4. Laminate. Two curved toughened panes are bonded with a structural interlayer, so the balustrade stays intact and in place even if a pane is damaged.
Step 15 — Balustrade, handrails & finishing

From a liability to a centrepiece

The curved glass is set into bespoke slotted brackets along the inner line, with continuous handrails to both sides — satisfying the compliance mitigation as well as the architecture. The stringers are clad to conceal every connection, and the steel is primed and painted to a selected RAL.
